ChatGPT’s Most Overused Words and Phrases (310+ List)

November 11, 2025
Key Takeaways
  • AI relies on predictable phrasing — the same intros, transitions, and shells show up repeatedly.
  • Overused connectors flatten flow and make sentences feel mechanical instead of human.
  • Buzzwords and action verbs add noise, not clarity, when they’re used as filler.
  • Vague modifiers inflate sentences without adding real meaning or specificity.
  • Abstract corporate nouns weaken impact because they replace concrete detail with fluff.
  • Stock templates expose AI rhythm, especially in explanations and structured paragraphs.
  • Metaphor tropes signal artificiality when they substitute drama for substance.
  • Generic conclusion patterns break immersion, because they summarize without insight.
  • Clearing these patterns restores your voice and makes the writing feel genuinely human.

ChatGPT writes by predicting statistically safe language.

When it isn’t given strong direction, it leans on the same recycled adjectives, polite filler, brochure-style verbs, and corporate jargon — the things that show up in almost every AI-generated draft. These patterns are exactly what people refer to when they talk about the “ChatGPT overused words and phrases problem”.

Our previous guide on the telltale signs of ChatGPT writing shows how these patterns reveal themselves. This article goes deeper. It gives you the complete, categorised reference list of the words and phrases that make writing sound generic, predictable, or machine-made.

Everything here is organised in clean sections and grid tables so you can scan, save, and remove these fingerprints from your work immediately.

Intros and Lead-Ins

Formulaic openers that instantly reveal AI-generated writing.

In the ever-evolving landscape of… In the ever-changing world of… In the rapidly shifting landscape of… In the constantly evolving field of… In the dynamic world of… In the modern age… In the current era… In the not-too-distant future…

Quickly flag the opening lines that make writing feel generic so you can replace them with sharper, more grounded starts.

Outros and Conclusion Patterns

Closing lines that rely on generic wrap-ups or summary scaffolding instead of actual final insight.

Clichéd Conclusion Openers
In conclusion… To summarize… To sum up… In closing… Ultimately… At the end of the day… In the final analysis… In brief…

Reflective and Summative Fillers
With that said… With all of this in mind… As we’ve seen… It’s clear that… All things considered… To wrap things up… All in all… In light of the above…

Inference and Takeaway Templates
Therefore, it is clear that… Thus, we can conclude that… As a result, the takeaway is… This highlights that… This underscores the idea that… We can infer that… This demonstrates that… This reinforces the point that…

Strip out automated-sounding wrap-ups and end with something specific, grounded, and tied to the actual content — not a template.

Stop Writing Like Default ChatGPT

Most of the issues in this guide come down to one thing: models fall back on whatever feels linguistically “safe”. That’s why the same meaningless buzzwords, transitions, templates, metaphors, and sentence shells show up again and again — because they’re statistically convenient.

Once you strip out those defaults, your writing changes.

It stops sounding generic.

It stops drifting into filler.

It stops revealing how it was made.
